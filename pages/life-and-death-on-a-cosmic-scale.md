Because funeral service places us face-to-face with life and death every day, it can sometimes be helpful to step back and look at the bigger picture — far beyond our immediate world.

**Let's look at the universe itself.**

The "**Pillars of Creation**" are towering columns of gas and dust located in the Eagle Nebula, roughly 7,000 light years from Earth. These massive formations are regions where new stars are being born. Sculpted by radiation and stellar winds, they are both beautiful and powerful — a reminder that creation often emerges from turbulence.

For astronomers, these pillars are a laboratory for studying how stars form. For the rest of us, they offer something quieter: perspective.

Inside stars, nuclear fusion creates the elements that make up the universe. Low-mass stars slowly fuse hydrogen into helium. Larger stars create heavier elements like carbon, oxygen, and silicon. The most massive stars eventually produce iron and beyond. When those massive stars explode as supernovae, they scatter even heavier elements — including gold and platinum — into space.

Those elements don't disappear.

**They become part of new stars.**
**New planets.**
**And eventually, life.**

In a very real sense, the atoms that make up our bodies were forged in ancient stars. The calcium in our bones, the iron in our blood, the oxygen we breathe — all of it began in stellar cores billions of years ago.

When we talk about life and death in funeral service, we are often speaking about something deeply personal and immediate. But on a cosmic scale, life is part of a continuous cycle. Energy changes form. Matter transforms. Nothing is truly lost — it becomes something new.

That perspective doesn't remove grief.
But sometimes it softens it.

Seeing life in the context of the universe reminds us that every person is part of something far larger than themselves. Each life is a brief but meaningful expression of matter and energy that has existed since the beginning of time.

**And in that sense, endings are not simply endings.**
**They are transitions.**

Sometimes stepping back to see the larger picture can bring unexpected peace.
