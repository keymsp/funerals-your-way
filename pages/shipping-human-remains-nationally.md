## Transporting a Body Across State Lines

When a death occurs in one state and final services are planned in another, the process is referred to as **interstate transportation of human remains**. Interstate transportation—sometimes referred to as domestic repatriation—requires coordination between licensed funeral homes, proper permits, and secure ground or air transfer. Moving a deceased person across state lines involves compliance with state-specific transit permit regulations and careful coordination with the receiving funeral home.

If a loved one's passing involves the [San Diego Medical Examiner](/san-diego-medical-examiner/), transportation—whether within California or to another state—will also require coordinated release authorization.

![Transporting deceased across the country](https://funeralsyourway.com/wp-content/uploads/transporting-body-across-the-country.webp)

Each state has its own documentation rules. Before transport can occur, certified death certificates and a transit or disposition permit must be issued. If air transportation is used, the shipping funeral home must hold "**known shipper**" status with the airline and comply with TSA security requirements. The receiving funeral home must also be prepared to accept custody and complete any local paperwork.

Ground transportation may be appropriate for shorter distances, while air transport is typically used for long-distance or cross-country transfers. Regardless of the method, proper preparation ensures the process is handled legally, respectfully, and without delay.

Families often ask how to move a loved one to another state quickly and safely. The key is working with an experienced funeral provider who manages permits, airline communication, packaging requirements, and coordination with the receiving facility so that nothing is overlooked.

## Is Embalming Required for Domestic Transportation of Human Remains?

**Embalming is not automatically required** when transporting a deceased person within the state or to another state. Requirements depend primarily on the method of transportation and the time between death and final disposition.

For air transportation, many airlines require embalming or the use of an approved hermetically sealed container to meet health and safety regulations. When ground transportation is used and transfer occurs within an appropriate timeframe, refrigeration or other preservation methods may be acceptable in compliance with state law.

Each state has its own preparation and transit permit requirements. The receiving funeral home may also have specific policies before accepting custody. ## Traveling With Cremated Remains by Air (Ashes)

When traveling with cremains (cremated remains) in the United States, it is **best to keep the urn in your carry-on bag, if possible**. Keeping the urn with you means you avoid the small chance but sometimes real risk of checked luggage being lost or delayed by the airline.

Cremated remains must be in a container that can be scanned by X-ray. The urn that you get from the crematory or the temporary plastic container that they give you complies with TSA rules and is used frequently for air travel. The security may look at the registry, but **the urn won't be opened**. Check with the airline because the policies may differ.

![Hispanic family going through TSA with cremains](https://funeralsyourway.com/wp-content/uploads/hispanic-family-going-through-tsa-with-cremains.webp)

### What if The Urn Cannot Be X-rayed

If the urn is made out of certain materials (like some metal, stone, or dense ceramic), then it may not be X-rayable. In these situations, the airline **may move the urn to and from checked luggage**. In these situations, the container may go through additional screenings, like checking for explosive residue before the travel is cleared.

### Necessary Documentation

We advise customers to keep the following documents to facilitate the screening process, regardless of whether the cremains are carried on or checked:

- Cremation Certificate from the crematory
- Certified Death Certificate
- Funeral Home Disposition/Transit Permit

While not required by TSA, having the above documentation on hand may provide further clarity for screeners and help avoid delays in the screening process.

## Frequently Asked Questions Regarding Shipping

### Is it possible to ship cremated remains in the United States?

Yes. When properly documented and packaged, cremated remains can be shipped to any location within the United States. Funeral homes regularly coordinate domestic shipping of cremated remains for families.

### How can cremated remains be mailed within the U.S.?

Cremated remains must be placed inside a sealed inner container and shipped using a USPS-approved Cremated Remains Kit. These kits are specifically designed for mailing ashes safely and in compliance with postal regulations.

### What paperwork is required to ship cremated remains domestically?

In most cases, a disposition or transit permit is required. While not always mandatory, many families also include a cremation certificate for additional clarification during transit.

### Can full-body remains be transported across state lines?

Yes. Licensed funeral homes may transport full-body remains interstate. This process requires certified death certificates, transit permits, and coordination between the shipping and receiving funeral homes.

### How is full-body transportation handled within the United States?

The shipping funeral home arranges secure ground or air transportation and prepares all necessary documentation. They coordinate directly with the receiving funeral home or cemetery to ensure compliance with all applicable regulations and respectful handling throughout the transfer.

### How long does it take to transport remains within the country?

Domestic transportation timelines vary based on distance, permit processing, and airline or ground availability. Most transfers are completed within several days once required documentation has been issued.

### Can family members travel with cremated remains on an airplane?

Yes. Cremated remains may be transported on domestic flights in carry-on luggage. The urn must be capable of being screened by X-ray and must comply with TSA and airline requirements.

### What documentation should families carry when traveling with cremated remains?

To help avoid delays at airport security, families should carry the cremation certificate, a certified copy of the death certificate, and any transit or disposition permits issued by the funeral home.
