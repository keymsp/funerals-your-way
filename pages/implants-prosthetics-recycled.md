Medical technology has advanced significantly over the years. Today, many individuals have implanted medical devices such as pacemakers, hip replacements, artificial joints, or prosthetic limbs. While some materials may be recyclable or considered more [environmentally friendly](/green-burials-preserving-the-earth/), families are often more concerned with a practical question: what happens to these devices after death?

When planning funeral arrangements, it is natural to wonder whether these man-made parts can remain in place or if they must be removed. Can they be **[cremated](/cremation/)**? Are any components ever recycled? The answer depends on the type of device and the method of disposition, and understanding the process can bring clarity during a difficult time.

## Do Pacemakers Need to Be Removed Before Cremation?

Yes. Pacemakers, implantable cardioverter defibrillators (ICDs), and other **battery-powered medical devices must be removed before cremation**. These devices contain batteries that can rupture under extreme heat, creating a safety risk during the cremation process. Families choosing [cremation services in San Diego](/cremation/) can feel confident that this step is handled safely and professionally.

![Pacemakers are removed before cremation](https://funeralsyourway.com/wp-content/uploads/pacemakers-are-removed-before-cremation.webp)

The removal is a simple procedure performed by a qualified professional prior to cremation. Families do not need to arrange this separately — **your funeral provider coordinates the process** to ensure everything is handled safely and respectfully.

Once removed, some organizations collect certain devices for humanitarian reuse in developing countries, where refurbished pacemakers can help patients who cannot afford new equipment. However, reuse programs are subject to medical regulations and vary by organization.

For families choosing cremation, this step is routine and carefully managed to ensure compliance with crematory safety standards.

## What Happens to Metal Implants Like Hip Replacements?

Unlike battery-powered devices, most metal implants — such as hip replacements, knee joints, surgical screws, or titanium rods — do not need to be removed prior to cremation. These materials do not burn during the cremation process because they are designed to withstand extremely high temperatures.

After cremation is complete, any remaining metal fragments are carefully separated from the cremated remains. Many modern crematories use specialized equipment to collect and process these materials.

**Depending on the facility, recovered metals may be:**

- Recycled through certified metal recovery programs
- Returned to families upon request
- Disposed of in accordance with local regulations

Some recycling programs donate proceeds to charitable organizations, turning recovered materials into something that benefits others.

For families, this process is handled professionally and respectfully. You do not need to make special arrangements unless you have specific wishes regarding the metal components.

## Can Prosthetic Limbs or Medical Devices Be Donated?

In some cases, yes. Certain external prosthetic limbs may be donated to nonprofit organizations that refurbish and redistribute them to individuals in need. One example is [Standing With Hope](https://www.standingwithhope.com/), which collects used prosthetic limbs and sends them to clinics in developing countries where they are refitted for new patients.

Internal implanted devices are more regulated. In the United States, re-implanting previously used medical devices is generally restricted by law. However, some humanitarian programs collect removed pacemakers and defibrillators for evaluation and possible reuse overseas. Organizations such as [Pace4Life](https://www.pace4life.org/) and [Project My Heart Your Heart](https://www.myheartyourheart.org/) work to provide refurbished cardiac devices to patients in underserved regions.

It's important to note that donation programs vary based on medical guidelines, regulatory approval, and the condition of the device. If a family expresses interest in donation, arrangements must be coordinated in advance to ensure compliance and proper handling.

For many families, simply understanding that there are options — whether recycling, charitable programs, or respectful disposal — provides reassurance during funeral planning.
