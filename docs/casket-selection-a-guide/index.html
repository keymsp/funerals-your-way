Selecting a Casket: A Comprehensive Guide

Selecting a casket is one of the most important decisions families make during funeral planning. It is both a practical choice and a personal one, influenced by budget, burial or cremation plans, cemetery requirements, and the wishes of the person being honored.

For many families, the casket is associated with a traditional ground burial, where it serves as both a ceremonial and final resting container. In other situations—such as cremation—families may select a cremation casket designed specifically for use in the cremation process or choose a rental option for services and viewing.

With a wide range of materials, designs, sizes, and price points available, the selection process can feel overwhelming. This guide explains the different types of caskets, common materials and features, cost considerations, and options for burial and cremation. Understanding these factors can help families make informed decisions that are respectful, appropriate, and aligned with their needs and values.

Understanding What a Casket Is

A casket is a container designed to hold the body of a deceased person during visitation, funeral services, and, in the case of burial, final interment. Caskets are most commonly used for traditional ground burial, but they may also be used temporarily for services prior to cremation.

Most caskets are constructed from wood or metal and feature a hinged lid, interior lining, and handles to allow for dignified presentation and transport. While caskets are often associated with burial, families choosing cremation may still select a casket for viewing or services, or use a specially designed cremation casket that is suitable for the cremation process.

It’s important to understand that the purpose of a casket can vary depending on the type of service being planned. For some families, the casket serves as a permanent resting place in a cemetery. For others, it is part of the ceremonial aspect of saying goodbye, with the final disposition taking place through cremation or another option.

Knowing how and when a casket is used helps families make informed decisions that align with their needs, values, and budget.

Cost of Caskets and Budget Considerations

One of the first considerations when selecting a casket is cost. Casket prices can vary widely based on material, construction, design details, and customization. Understanding what affects pricing can help families make informed decisions without unnecessary pressure.

In general, casket prices may range from around $1,000 to several thousand dollars, depending on the options selected. Premium materials such as bronze, copper, or high-end hardwoods tend to be the most expensive, while simpler metal, wood, or alternative-material caskets are more budget-friendly. Many families choose options in the mid-range that balance appearance, durability, and cost.

It’s also important to know that federal law allows families to purchase a casket from any retailer they choose, not just the funeral home. This flexibility gives families control over their budget and ensures they can compare options comfortably.

When evaluating cost, families should consider:

  • Material and thickness (metal vs. wood, hardwood vs. softwood)
  • Interior fabric and finish
  • Added features such as memory drawers, decorative hardware, or gasket seals
  • Cemetery requirements, including whether a vault is required

A licensed funeral director can help explain pricing clearly, review available options, and ensure that the selected casket meets cemetery requirements—helping families stay within budget while making a choice that feels appropriate and respectful.

We recommend purchasing your casket from the funeral home you plan with—if possible. This is easier, and you can get customizations and support a local business. It is also far easier to return a damaged casket for an immediate replacement should the need arise.

Casket Materials and Eco-Friendly Options

Caskets are available in a wide range of materials, each offering different aesthetic, durability, and cost considerations. The most common materials include metal and wood, though environmentally conscious alternatives are also available.

Metal caskets are typically made from steel, copper, or bronze and are valued for their strength and uniform appearance. Steel caskets are available in different gauges, which affects thickness and price. Copper and bronze options are less common and tend to be significantly more expensive.

Wood caskets are often chosen for their natural appearance and warmth. Common wood types include pine, poplar, oak, maple, and mahogany. Pricing varies depending on the type of wood and level of craftsmanship, with hardwoods generally costing more than softwoods.

Some families planning environmentally conscious services, including green burial, may prefer caskets made from biodegradable or minimally processed materials. These can include wicker, bamboo, cardboard, or unfinished wood. Availability and acceptance of these materials depend on cemetery or burial ground requirements, so it’s important to confirm what is permitted in advance.

In addition to exterior materials, caskets also differ in interior fabrics and finishes, which can range from simple natural linings to more decorative materials. Selecting materials that align with personal values, environmental preferences, and burial plans can help families feel confident in their choice.

Casket Sizes and Dimensions

Selecting the correct casket size is an important part of funeral planning and helps ensure a dignified and respectful presentation during services and burial.

Most standard adult caskets are designed to accommodate a wide range of body sizes and typically measure approximately 24–27 inches wide, 75–83 inches long, and 23–27 inches high. These dimensions are suitable for many individuals, but they are not universal.

For individuals who are taller or have a larger body frame, oversized caskets are available. These are designed to provide appropriate space and comfort while maintaining the same appearance and function as standard caskets. Oversized options are commonly requested and are routinely accommodated by cemeteries and funeral homes.

When burial is planned, cemeteries often require advance notice of the casket’s exterior dimensions so the grave can be properly prepared. Confirming size requirements in advance helps avoid complications on the day of the service and ensures the burial proceeds smoothly.

A funeral professional can assist in confirming the appropriate casket size based on individual needs and cemetery requirements, helping families feel confident that practical details are handled with care.

Casket Design and Personalization

Casket design and personalization allow families to reflect the personality, values, and life of the person being honored. While some families prefer a simple, traditional appearance, others choose design elements that feel more personal or symbolic.

Caskets are available in a wide range of styles, finishes, and colors, from understated and classic to more decorative designs. Wood caskets may feature natural finishes or detailed craftsmanship, while metal caskets often offer a variety of exterior finishes and interior accents.

Personalization options can include:

  • Interior fabric and lining choices
  • Embroidered head panels or interior messages
  • Decorative hardware or handles
  • Memorial features such as small keepsake compartments, where permitted

These options are entirely optional. Choosing a simpler design does not diminish the dignity or meaning of a service, just as personalization does not need to be elaborate to feel meaningful. What matters most is that the selection feels appropriate and reflective of the individual being remembered.

A funeral professional can explain which design and personalization options are available and appropriate based on burial or cremation plans, cemetery requirements, and budget considerations.

Cremation Caskets and Rental Options

When cremation is planned, families still need to decide how their loved one will be presented during any visitation or service. In these cases, a cremation casket or a rental casket may be used.

Cremation caskets are specifically designed to be suitable for the cremation process. They are typically made from combustible materials such as wood, fiberboard, or reinforced cardboard and do not contain metal components that would interfere with cremation. These caskets may have a simpler construction, but they are appropriate for services and viewing prior to cremation.

Some families choose a rental casket, which includes a removable interior container. This option allows for a traditional appearance during visitation or services while using a separate cremation container for the cremation itself. Rental caskets are often selected as a more economical option when a viewing or ceremony is desired.

Funeral homes may charge an additional fee related to the cremation of a casket or cremation container, depending on the materials used and the crematory’s requirements. A funeral professional can explain these details in advance so families understand what to expect.

Understanding the difference between burial caskets, cremation caskets, and rental options helps families choose an arrangement that aligns with their service plans, preferences, and budget.
