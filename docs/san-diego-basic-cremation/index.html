Basic Cremation vs. Direct Cremation

Why Families Choose Direct Cremation

Direct cremation is a type of cremation service that involves the cremation of a deceased person’s body most generally without any funeral ceremony or visitation. A short viewing is still possible for the immediate family if they desire it. A Memorial Service is also a smart idea, so for lots of information, see our San Diego Memorial Services page.

Direct cremation is becoming increasingly popular because it is generally less expensive than a traditional funeral with a viewing or visitation. This is because fewer services are involved.

Direct Cremation – The Process Details

Cremation is a common method of disposition of human remains around the world. It involves exposing the deceased body to extreme heat in a specialized furnace known as a cremator or retort. The intense heat of the cremator reduces the body to its basic elements, which are known as cremated remains or ashes. The cremation process is a precise and detailed procedure that requires a special Cremator’s licence from the state funeral board.

The cremation process can be broken down into four main steps: preparing the body, placing the body in the cremator, cremating the body, and processing the cremated remains.

The cremation process itself typically takes between three and four hours, although timing can vary depending on the individual and the equipment used.

Direct Cremation – Body Preparation

Before the cremation process can begin, the body must be prepared. This involves removing any jewelry, pacemakers, or other medical devices that could interfere with the cremation process. The body is then placed in a cremation container, which is usually made of wood, cardboard, or other combustible materials. The container is designed to protect the body and ensure that it is cremated properly.

Cremation – Placing the body in the cremator (Retort)

Once the body is prepared, it is placed in the cremator. The cremator is a specialized furnace that is designed to withstand the extreme heat required for cremation.

The cremator’s door is then sealed and the unit is heated to a temperature of between 1,600 and 1,800 degrees Fahrenheit.

Most crematories use either natural gas or liquefied petroleum gas, although electric retorts are used in some locations outside the United States.

Cremating the Body

Once the body is in the cremator, the actual cremation process begins. The heat of the cremator causes the body to break down into its basic elements. This process takes between two and three hours, depending on the size of the body and the type of cremator used.

During the cremation process, the body is subjected to a process known as pyrolysis. Pyrolysis is the chemical decomposition of organic materials through the application of heat. During this process, the body is broken down into its basic elements, including carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen. The bones of the body are also reduced to their basic elements, which are known as cremated remains or ashes.

The amount of cremains created does vary from person to person. In general, there are 4-6 lbs of cremains, but this varies slightly depending on the size of the person. Size, in this case, does not so much reflect weight as skeletal mass as the soft tissues, such as fat, are consumed during the cremation process.

Cremation – Processing the Cremated Remains

Once the cremation process is complete, the cremated remains are removed from the cremator. The remains are then processed to ensure that they are fine and uniform in texture. Any metal fragments, such as dental fillings or surgical implants, are removed using a magnet or other specialized equipment. The cremated remains are then placed in a temporary container, such as a plastic bag or cardboard box. The container is labeled with the name of the deceased and any other relevant information.

It is worth noting that the cremation process itself is only one part of the overall process of disposition of human remains. Once the cremation process is complete, the cremated remains can be interred in a cemetery, scattered in a designated area, or kept by the family in an urn or other container.

Why Do Cremated Remains Vary in Color?

The color of cremains, the fine powder that remains after the cremation process, can be influenced by several factors. While the color is often a light gray or white, variations can occur due to the following factors.

  • Bone Composition — The natural composition and density of a person’s bones can influence the appearance of cremated remains. Younger individuals often have denser bone structure, while age and certain medical conditions may result in slight variations in color.
  • Cremation Temperature — Higher temperatures and longer cremation cycles may produce lighter and more uniform cremated remains. Lower temperatures or shorter cycles can sometimes create slight differences in appearance.
  • Medical Implants — Metal or ceramic implants do not completely combust during cremation. While these materials are removed during processing, they can occasionally influence the appearance or texture of the cremated remains.
  • Final Processing — After cremation, the remains are carefully processed to create a consistent texture. The equipment and methods used may affect the final appearance, resulting in cremated remains that are finer or more uniform.

Frequently Asked Questions About Direct Cremation

How long does the cremation process take in San Diego?

The cremation process itself usually takes three to four hours, depending on the individual (larger individuals take longer) and the equipment used (some cremation chambers take half this time). The death certificate MUST be completed before cremation can happen. The total time for us taking a loved one into our care to the family having the cremains back is generally about 10 days – there is some variability.

What is included in your basic / direct cremation package?

Our Basic / Direct Cremation Package includes transportation of your loved one into our care, completion of all legal paperwork, cremation at a licensed facility, a wooden urn (if you are not scattering), the disposition permit, and notification to Social Security. We also plant a memorial tree in honor of your loved one through the national forest service.

Do we need to buy an urn separately?

A wooden urn (or basic plastic urn if you are scattering) is included with every cremation. Families may choose to purchase a more decorative or biodegradable urn (we have hundreds) and can help you select one appropriate for your family.

Can we have a viewing before the cremation?

Yes. A brief family viewing can be arranged before the cremation takes place. This is something we really recommend if possible as it really helps with healing and closure. Our staff will help schedule a private, respectful farewell if that’s important to your family.

Can we still hold a memorial service after the cremation?

Yes, of course. Many families choose to hold a memorial service, celebration of life, or ash scattering at sea after the cremation. Having a cremation helps with not being tied to a fast time schedule. We can help you plan a ceremony that’s meaningful and personalized.

How are the ashes returned to the family?

Most families choose to pick up the cremains from our office. You can also arrange to have them delivered to your residence by our staff or have them shipped to you by USPS Priority Mail Express with full tracking – you would generally receive the cremains in a couple days.

What should we do with the ashes after cremation?

Families often choose to scatter ashes at sea (for which we have a whole separate company – SanDiegoBurialatSea.com), keep them in an urn at home, place them in a niche, or create keepsakes or jewelry. We can help you explore all available options.

Is direct cremation the same as basic cremation?

Yes. In California, direct cremation is commonly referred to as basic cremation. Both terms describe cremation without a traditional viewing or funeral service, while still allowing families to hold a memorial or celebration of life at a later time.

Is direct cremation a respectful option?

Absolutely. Direct cremation is a dignified and respectful choice when handled by a licensed funeral home. The level of care and professionalism remains the same, regardless of whether a traditional service is held.
