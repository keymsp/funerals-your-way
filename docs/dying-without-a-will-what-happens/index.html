What Happens If You Die Without a Will in California?

When someone dies without a valid will, they are considered to have died “intestate.” In California, this means state law — not personal wishes — determines how property and assets are distributed. The court oversees the probate process, appoints a representative to manage the estate, and distributes assets according to California’s intestate succession laws. Understanding what happens without a will can help families avoid unnecessary delays, costs, and confusion during an already difficult time.

How Intestate Succession Works in California

When someone dies without a valid will, California’s intestate succession laws determine who inherits their property. These rules apply automatically and follow a specific order based on surviving family members.

In general:

  • If there is a surviving spouse but no children or parents, the spouse typically inherits the estate.
  • If there are children but no spouse, the children inherit the estate equally.
  • If there is a spouse and children, the spouse usually inherits all community property and a portion of separate property, while the children inherit the remaining separate property.
  • If there is no spouse or children, parents, siblings, or more distant relatives may inherit according to state law.

Community property and separate property are treated differently under California law, which can affect how assets are divided. Because every family situation is different, distribution may vary depending on who survives the decedent.

Does the Estate Have to Go Through Probate?

In most cases, yes. When someone dies without a will in California, the estate must go through probate unless assets were structured to pass outside of court.

Probate is a court-supervised process where a judge appoints an administrator to manage the estate. This person is responsible for identifying assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing property according to California’s intestate succession laws.

The probate process can take several months — and in some cases more than a year — depending on the size and complexity of the estate. Court filings, legal notices, and required waiting periods are all part of the process.

Because probate is public and can involve legal fees and administrative costs, many families choose to plan ahead through wills or living trusts to simplify matters for their loved ones.

Which Assets Do Not Go Through Probate?

Not all property must pass through probate court. Certain assets transfer automatically to a beneficiary or co-owner, even if there is no will.

In California, the following assets typically avoid probate:

  • Property held in a living trust
  • Life insurance policies with a named beneficiary
  • Retirement accounts (such as IRAs or 401(k)s) with designated beneficiaries
  • Payable-on-death (POD) bank accounts
  • Transfer-on-death (TOD) securities accounts
  • Vehicles registered with transfer-on-death designation
  • Property owned in joint tenancy or as community property with right of survivorship

These assets transfer directly to the named beneficiary or surviving owner without court involvement.

One of the most common estate planning tools used to avoid probate is a revocable living trust. A living trust is created during a person’s lifetime and can be modified as circumstances change. It allows assets to be distributed privately and often more efficiently after death.

Who Manages the Estate?

If there is no will, the probate court appoints an administrator to handle the estate. This is usually a spouse or close family member.

The administrator is responsible for gathering assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing property according to California law. Unlike an executor named in a will, the administrator must follow the court’s supervision and state intestate succession rules.

Why Planning Ahead Matters

Dying without a will does not mean chaos — but it does mean that the state decides how assets are distributed rather than the individual. For some families, the outcome may align with their wishes. For others, it may not.

Creating a will or establishing a trust allows you to decide how assets are distributed and can reduce delays for your family during an already difficult time. Families planning ahead can also review our Funeral Planning Services for additional guidance.

While this article provides general information about California intestate succession and probate, estate planning decisions should always be discussed with a qualified attorney. Every family situation is different, and proper planning can provide clarity and peace of mind. Frequently Asked Questions

Families often have additional questions about probate, intestate succession, and what happens when someone dies without a will in California. The answers below address some of the most common concerns.

What happens if someone dies without a will in California?

If a person dies without a will in California, their estate is distributed according to California intestate succession laws. The probate court appoints an administrator to manage the estate and distribute assets to heirs as defined by state law.

Does an estate have to go through probate if there is no will?

In most cases, yes. When someone dies without a will, the estate must go through probate unless assets were structured to pass outside of court, such as through a living trust or named beneficiary designations.

Who manages the estate if there is no will?

If there is no will, the probate court appoints an administrator. This person is usually a spouse or close family member and is responsible for gathering assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing property according to California law.

Which assets do not go through probate in California?

Assets that typically avoid probate include property held in a living trust, life insurance with a named beneficiary, retirement accounts with designated beneficiaries, payable-on-death bank accounts, transfer-on-death securities, and property owned in joint tenancy with right of survivorship.

How can probate be avoided in California?

Probate may be avoided by using estate planning tools such as a revocable living trust, beneficiary designations, payable-on-death accounts, and joint ownership structures. Families should consult a qualified estate planning attorney for guidance specific to their situation.
