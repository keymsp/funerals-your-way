California End of Life Option Act (Right to Die Law Explained)

California’s End of Life Option Act allows certain terminally ill adults to request medical aid in dying under specific legal safeguards. Often referred to as the “Right to Die” law, this legislation provides eligible individuals with the option to make deeply personal end-of-life decisions in accordance with strict medical and procedural requirements. Understanding who qualifies, how the process works, and what safeguards are in place can help individuals and families approach this sensitive topic with clarity and informed judgment. Families often explore options such as palliative care and hospice care when facing serious illness.

What Is the California End of Life Option Act?

The California End of Life Option Act is a state law that allows certain terminally ill adults to request and receive medication intended to peacefully end their life. The law took effect in 2016 after being signed by Governor Jerry Brown in 2015.

The Act applies only to individuals who:

  • Are at least 18 years old
  • Are residents of California
  • Have been diagnosed with a terminal illness expected to result in death within six months
  • Are mentally capable of making medical decisions
  • Are able to self-administer the prescribed medication

The law includes multiple safeguards designed to ensure that the decision is voluntary, informed, and free from coercion. Two physicians must confirm the diagnosis and eligibility requirements before medication can be prescribed.

The End of Life Option Act does not require any doctor, hospital, or religious medical system to participate. Participation is entirely voluntary for both patients and healthcare providers.

I do not know what I would do if I were dying in prolonged and excruciating pain. I am certain, however, that it would be a comfort to be able to consider the options afforded by the bill. And I wouldn’t deny the right to others.

— Jerry Brown, California Governor

Eligibility Requirements Under the Law

Not every serious illness qualifies under the End of Life Option Act. The law applies only to individuals who meet specific medical and legal criteria.

To qualify, a person must:

  • Be diagnosed with a terminal illness that is expected to lead to death within six months
  • Be mentally capable of making and communicating health care decisions
  • Be able to make two verbal requests to a physician, separated by a required waiting period
  • Submit a written request witnessed by qualified individuals
  • Be physically able to self-administer the medication

Mental competency is essential. If either physician believes the patient may be suffering from impaired judgment due to depression or another mental health condition, a referral for a psychological evaluation may be required.

The law is designed to ensure that the decision is voluntary, informed, and made without pressure from family members or others.

Safeguards and Legal Protections Built Into the Law

The End of Life Option Act includes multiple safeguards designed to ensure that decisions are voluntary, informed, and free from coercion.

These protections include:

  • Confirmation of diagnosis and prognosis by two independent physicians
  • Verification that the patient has the mental capacity to make medical decisions
  • Required waiting periods between requests
  • A written request signed by qualified witnesses
  • The ability for the patient to withdraw their request at any time

No physician, pharmacist, hospital, or healthcare system is required to participate. Many religious-based institutions choose not to provide this service, and patients may need to seek providers who voluntarily participate.

Additionally, the law requires that the medication be self-administered. This provision is intended to ensure that the final act remains the patient’s independent choice.

These safeguards are meant to balance personal autonomy with careful oversight, ensuring that the process is thoughtful, deliberate, and legally compliant.

Common Misunderstandings About the Right to Die Law

The End of Life Option Act is often misunderstood. It does not allow euthanasia, and it does not permit anyone else to administer medication. The patient must be mentally competent and must self-administer the prescribed medication.

Choosing medical aid in dying is not considered suicide under California law. It is a medical option available only to qualified, terminally ill adults who meet strict eligibility requirements.

It is also important to understand that many patients who complete the approval process never use the medication. For some, simply having the option provides a sense of control and peace of mind during an uncertain time.

The law is designed to respect individual autonomy while maintaining strong medical and legal safeguards.

Planning Ahead Brings Clarity and Peace of Mind

Conversations about end-of-life care can be difficult, but planning ahead often reduces uncertainty and stress for loved ones. Whether a person chooses traditional medical treatment, hospice care, or wishes to explore the End of Life Option Act, documenting preferences in advance ensures that decisions reflect personal values.

Advance directives, health care proxies, and thoughtful funeral preplanning allow individuals to communicate their wishes clearly. When arrangements are made in advance, families are able to focus on support, time together, and honoring those wishes rather than navigating urgent decisions during a crisis. Families may also benefit from reviewing our Funeral Question & Answer page for guidance.

For many families, planning ahead is not about expecting the worst — it is about providing guidance, dignity, and peace of mind for the future.

Frequently Asked Questions About California’s Right to Die Law

Because the End of Life Option Act involves medical, legal, and ethical considerations, many families have important questions about how the law works. Below are answers to common concerns regarding eligibility, safeguards, and the decision-making process.

Who qualifies under California’s End of Life Option Act?

Only adults (18 or older) who are California residents, diagnosed with a terminal illness expected to result in death within six months, and who are mentally capable of making medical decisions may qualify.

Does a doctor have to participate?

No. Participation is voluntary for physicians, pharmacists, hospitals, and healthcare systems. Some providers and religious-based institutions choose not to participate.

Is this considered assisted suicide?

Under California law, medical aid in dying provided through the End of Life Option Act is not classified as suicide. It is a legal medical option available to qualified patients.

Can a patient change their mind?

Yes. A patient may withdraw their request at any time, even after receiving the medication.

A Personal and Private Decision

Decisions involving serious illness and end-of-life care are deeply personal. The End of Life Option Act provides a legal framework for those who qualify, but it is only one part of a broader conversation about comfort, dignity, and individual values.

Every family’s situation is unique. Some individuals prioritize extending life as long as possible, while others focus on comfort and quality of life. What matters most is that decisions are made thoughtfully, with clear communication between patients, loved ones, and medical professionals.

Understanding available options — including palliative care, hospice care, advance directives, and preplanning — allows individuals to approach the future with greater clarity and peace of mind. Some families may ultimately choose cremation, while others prefer traditional burial, depending on personal, cultural, or religious preferences.
