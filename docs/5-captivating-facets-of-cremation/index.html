5 Important Cremation Facts Families Should Know

Cremation has become the most common form of disposition in California, yet many families still have questions about how the process works, what options are available, and what happens after cremation is complete. When a loss occurs, decisions often need to be made quickly, and clear information isn’t always easy to find during an emotional time.

At Funerals Your Way, a fully licensed San Diego funeral home, we believe families deserve straightforward explanations without pressure or confusion. Understanding the basics of cremation—from containers and remains to memorial options and environmental considerations—can help families feel more confident and at ease as they move forward.

The following five cremation facts address the questions we are asked most often and are intended to provide clarity, reassurance, and practical guidance for families considering cremation services in San Diego.

Cremation Uses Alternative Containers (Not Traditional Caskets)

When cremation is selected, a traditional hardwood or metal casket is not required. Instead, California law allows the use of an alternative cremation container, which is specifically designed for cremation and meets all state and crematory requirements.

Most basic cremation services use containers made from corrugated fiberboard or reinforced cardboard, as shown above. These containers are fully enclosed, dignified, and structurally sound for transport and cremation. In some cases, interior supports or trays are included to provide added stability.

For many families, alternative containers offer a simple and economical option without compromising respect or care. Because they are made from lightweight materials, they also produce significantly fewer emissions during cremation when compared to hardwood caskets.

Families should also know that alternative containers can be:

  • Used with or without a viewing
  • Decorated or personalized with messages or artwork
  • Chosen as part of environmentally conscious arrangements

If a ceremonial casket is desired for a viewing or service, families may choose a rental casket, with the cremation taking place afterward using an approved alternative container.

What Happens to the Remains After Cremation

After cremation is complete, the remaining material is carefully processed before being returned to the family. While cremated remains are commonly referred to as “ashes,” they are primarily composed of bone fragments that remain after all organic material has been reduced through heat.

These fragments are respectfully placed into a specialized processing unit that reduces them into a fine, uniform consistency. This step is performed with care and in accordance with established professional standards before the remains are placed into a temporary container or urn selected by the family.

During this process, non-combustible materials—such as medical implants, metal fasteners, or jewelry remnants—are removed. These items are separated in compliance with crematory procedures and applicable regulations.

Once returned to the family, cremated remains may be:

  • Placed in an urn for permanent keeping
  • Buried or placed in a columbarium
  • Scattered in an approved outdoor location
  • Used in memorial items such as glass art or planting tributes

Understanding this part of the cremation process often helps families feel more comfortable and informed when making decisions during a difficult time.

Scattering at Sea as a Cremation Memorial Option

Scattering cremated remains at sea has become a meaningful memorial option for many families, particularly in coastal communities like San Diego. This type of ceremony offers a peaceful setting that allows loved ones to gather, reflect, and honor a life in a natural and unhurried way.

A sea scattering typically takes place aboard a vessel that travels offshore to approved ocean locations. The ceremony can be simple and private or include readings, music, flowers, or personal remarks shared by family members. Many families find the open water and rhythm of the ocean to be calming and supportive during the farewell.

Scattering at sea is also an environmentally mindful option when performed using biodegradable materials and in accordance with federal and state regulations. These guidelines help ensure the protection of marine environments while allowing families to return their loved one to nature with care and respect.

At Funerals Your Way, we assist families with all arrangements for sea scattering, including coordination, permits, and ceremony planning. Each service is personal, and no two ceremonies are ever the same. Families are encouraged to participate at their own comfort level, whether attending in person or arranging a private release. Some families find it helpful to see examples of past ceremonies to better understand what a sea scattering looks like.

For many, scattering at sea offers a lasting sense of peace—knowing their loved one has become part of the ocean they cherished or felt connected to.

Cremation Jewelry and Personal Memorial Options

After cremation, many families look for meaningful ways to keep a loved one close or create a lasting tribute. Cremation jewelry and other personal memorial options allow families to honor remembrance in a way that feels private, symbolic, and personal.

Cremation jewelry is designed to hold a small portion of cremated remains within a discreet compartment. Common options include necklaces, pendants, rings, bracelets, and lockets, available in a wide range of styles and materials. Some pieces can be engraved, while others incorporate gemstones or artistic elements chosen by the family.

In addition to jewelry, cremated remains may be incorporated into:

  • Hand-blown glass memorials
  • Keepsake urns
  • Memorial artwork
  • Planting tributes, such as trees or flowers grown from memorial vessels

These options allow families to choose how visible or private their remembrance feels. Some prefer a wearable tribute, while others select a keepsake that can be displayed at home or shared among family members.

Personal memorial choices are entirely optional and deeply individual. Families may choose one option, several, or none at all, depending on what feels right for them.

Environmental Considerations of the Cremation Process

Cremation is often viewed as a more flexible and space-conscious alternative to traditional burial, and for many families, environmental impact is part of the decision-making process. Like any method of disposition, cremation involves energy use and emissions that are important to understand.

During cremation, carbon dioxide (CO₂) is released from two primary sources. The first is the combustion of the body’s organic material. The second comes from the fuel required to operate cremation equipment at the high temperatures necessary for the process. The amount of CO₂ produced can vary based on factors such as equipment efficiency, fuel type, and individual circumstances.

Advancements in cremation technology have helped reduce environmental impact over time. Modern cremation chambers are designed to operate more efficiently, with improved temperature controls and filtration systems that help minimize emissions while meeting regulatory standards.

For families who prioritize environmental considerations, there are additional ways to reduce impact, including:

  • Selecting simple alternative containers rather than hardwood caskets
  • Choosing memorial options that avoid permanent materials
  • Considering biodegradable urns or natural scattering practices

Understanding these factors allows families to weigh their options thoughtfully and choose a cremation approach that aligns with their values, preferences, and beliefs.

Frequently Asked Questions About Cremation

Families often have practical questions about cremation, especially when making arrangements during an emotional time. The following answers address some of the most common questions we receive and are intended to provide clarity, reassurance, and helpful guidance as you consider your options.

Is cremation allowed by all religions?

Many religions now permit cremation, including the Roman Catholic Church, which allows cremation provided it is not chosen for reasons contrary to faith. However, beliefs can vary by denomination and personal tradition. Families are encouraged to consult their religious leader or discuss their preferences with a funeral director when making arrangements.

Do families receive all of the cremated remains?

Yes. After cremation and processing are complete, the cremated remains are returned to the family in full. Any non-combustible materials, such as medical implants, are respectfully removed according to standard procedures before the remains are placed in an urn or temporary container.

Is a casket required for cremation?

No. Cremation does not require a traditional casket. Most cremations are performed using an approved alternative container made from reinforced cardboard or fiberboard, which meets all California and crematory requirements. Families may choose a rental casket if a viewing or service is desired before cremation.

Can cremated remains be scattered, kept, or divided among family members?

Yes. Cremated remains may be scattered in approved locations, kept in an urn, placed in a columbarium, or divided into keepsakes such as jewelry or small memorial urns. Families may choose one option or a combination, depending on what feels most meaningful to them.

Cremation Facts — In Summary

Cremation continues to grow in popularity because it offers flexibility, affordability, and a wide range of meaningful ways to honor a loved one. From the use of simple alternative containers, to how cremated remains are processed, memorialized, scattered at sea, or kept close through jewelry, cremation allows families to choose what feels right for them.

Understanding the cremation process ahead of time can ease uncertainty and provide peace of mind during a difficult moment. If you have questions about cremation in San Diego, our licensed funeral directors are available to explain your options clearly and without pressure.
