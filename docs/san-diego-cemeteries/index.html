San Diego Cemeteries – Public and Private

San Diego has 26 cemeteries, some public and some private. Cost of cemetery plots vary greatly.

There are both public and private cemeteries in the San Diego area. The city or a public governing authority generally owns the public cemeteries while corporations own the private ones.

In general, public cemeteries are significantly less expensive than private ones.

It depends on the graveyard, but often only individuals who live in the area of the cemetery are allowed to be buried there. If the cemetery is open to everyone there may be additional fees for people not from that area.

Public Cemeteries for Interment

Public cemeteries are government-owned burial grounds and often have unique features that distinguish them from private cemeteries.

Accessibility

Public cemeteries are open to all members of the public without any specific membership requirements or eligibility criteria. They provide burial services and plots to individuals and families from diverse backgrounds, regardless of religious, cultural, or social affiliations. Public cemeteries strive to be inclusive and accessible to the entire community.

Size and Capacity

Public cemeteries tend to be more extensive than many private cemeteries. They are often designed to accommodate more burials and provide ample space for future generations. Public cemeteries often have sections or designated areas for different religious groups, military veterans, or specific cultural communities.

Governance and Regulations

Public cemeteries are subject to governmental oversight and are governed by regulations and policies set by the relevant authorities. These regulations ensure standardized procedures for burials, maintenance, monument installation, and other cemetery-related activities. The governing authority ensures that public cemeteries comply with legal requirements and maintain appropriate standards of care.

Maintenance and Care

Public cemeteries typically receive regular maintenance and care from dedicated staff employed by the governing authority. Maintenance includes landscaping, mowing, tree maintenance, and general upkeep of the cemetery grounds. The aim is to create a well-maintained and serene environment that honors the memories of the deceased and provides a peaceful space for visitors.

Veterans’ Sections

Many public cemeteries have dedicated sections or areas specifically for military veterans. These sections often provide special honors and recognition for veterans, including markers, flags, and organized ceremonies during specific national holidays such as Memorial Day or Veterans Day.

Historical Significance

Public cemeteries often have historical significance and may contain the gravesites of notable individuals, community leaders, or historical figures. They can serve as repositories of local history, preserving the legacy and heritage of the community.

Public Facilities and Services

Public cemeteries may offer additional facilities and services to visitors. These can include visitor information centers, chapels for funeral services, mausoleums, columbaria for cremated remains, and designated areas for scattering ashes. Public cemeteries often provide clear signage, maps, and directories to assist visitors in locating gravesites and navigating the cemetery grounds.

Community Engagement

Public cemeteries often foster community engagement and participation. They may organize educational programs, guided tours, or events that promote cemetery history, genealogy research, or cultural heritage. Public cemeteries can serve as gathering places for communal activities and commemorative ceremonies.

These features contribute to the overall character and function of public cemeteries, reflecting their commitment to serving the broader community and honoring the memory of the departed in a respectful and inclusive manner.

Private Cemeteries for Interment

As distinct from public cemeteries, private cemeteries often possess unique features and characteristics that set them apart.

Restricted Access

It’s important to note that the specific characteristics of private cemeteries can vary widely depending on the cemetery’s location, ownership, and purpose. Each private cemetery may have distinct qualities and offerings that reflect the vision and values of the owners or members it serves.

Customized Burial Options

Private cemeteries often offer a range of customized burial options catering to the specific preferences of their members or owners. These options may include family plots, private mausoleums, designated sections for particular groups or families, or personalizing sites with custom markers or monuments.

Specialized Themes or Cultures

Some private cemeteries are designed to cater to specific cultural or religious communities. These cemeteries may incorporate unique features, symbols, or rituals significant to the particular culture or religion they serve. They provide a sense of identity and belonging to individuals or families with shared cultural or religious backgrounds.

Maintenance and Landscaping

Private cemeteries often prioritize maintenance and landscaping more than public cemeteries. The owners or governing entities may invest more resources and attention into preserving the cemetery’s appearance and ensuring its well-maintained condition. This investment can include beautifully landscaped gardens, manicured lawns, and regular upkeep of gravesites and markers.

Family or Community Heritage

Private cemeteries can hold deep historical and familial significance for the members or owners. They may be associated with a family’s ancestral lineage or have a long-standing connection to a specific community. These cemeteries often provide a sense of continuity, allowing families or communities to maintain a link to their past and honor their heritage.

Exclusive Services and Amenities

Private cemeteries may offer additional services and amenities not commonly found in public cemeteries. These can include on-site chapels or prayer rooms, reception facilities for memorial gatherings, grief counseling services, or exclusive events for cemetery members.

Preservation of Historic Elements

Some private cemeteries have historical significance due to the presence of notable individuals, unique architectural features, or cultural artifacts. The owners or governing entities of these cemeteries often prioritize preserving and protecting these historical elements, maintaining the cemetery as a valuable heritage site.

Burial Vault or Grave Liner

A casket vault, also known as a burial vault or grave liner, is a container that encloses a casket during burial. It is typically made of reinforced concrete, plastic, or metal. The primary purpose of a casket vault is to provide more protection and support to the casket and the surrounding soil.

Here are the main reasons why casket vaults are used:

  • Preservation and Protection: A casket vault helps protect the casket from the weight and pressure of the earth above. It acts as a sturdy outer shell, preventing the soil from directly contacting the casket. This protection helps to preserve the casket’s integrity and prevents the burial site from sinking or collapsing over time.
  • Preventing Ground Settling: Using a casket vault helps reduce the risk of the ground above the burial site settling or sinking. The vault provides a stable and reinforced base for the soil, minimizing the chances of the ground subsiding and maintaining the cemetery’s overall appearance.
  • Maintaining Cemetery Landscaping: Casket vaults help preserve the cemetery’s landscape by preventing soil disruption. Without a vault, the weight of the earth and heavy machinery used in cemetery maintenance could cause the ground to sink or become uneven, leading to potential damage to headstones, pathways, and other grave sites.
  • Groundwater Protection: Another essential function of a casket vault is protecting the casket and contents from groundwater intrusion. The vault acts as a barrier, helping prevent water from seeping into the burial site. The vault helps maintain the integrity of the casket and reduces the risk of contamination.

The use of casket vaults may vary depending on local regulations, cemetery policies, or personal preferences. Some cemeteries may require a vault for all burials, while others may leave it as an optional choice for families.
