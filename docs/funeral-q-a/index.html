Frequently Asked Funeral Questions

Have a question? Find answers below by topic or scroll to browse. If you come across unfamiliar terminology while planning, our Funeral Terms Glossary provides clear definitions to help you better understand the funeral process.

Our licensed funeral directors are available 24 hours a day to answer questions specific to your situation. Call 619-550-7807 any time, or contact us online.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is embalming required in California?

No. Embalming is not legally required in California in most situations. State law allows burial or cremation without embalming, especially when disposition takes place within a reasonable time frame. Embalming may be recommended if there will be a public viewing or if services are delayed for several days. Some religious traditions do not permit embalming at all.

How long after death must cremation occur in California?

In California, cremation can generally take place after required permits are issued and the legally mandated waiting period has passed. Most cremations occur within several days, depending on paperwork completion, doctor certification, and county authorization. San Diego County requires proper disposition permits before cremation proceeds.

Can we still have a service if we choose cremation?

Yes. Cremation is an alternative to burial, not an alternative to having a funeral service. Many San Diego families choose a viewing or traditional service before cremation, while others hold a memorial service afterward with the cremated remains present. Choosing cremation provides flexibility in how and when services are held.

How much does cremation cost in San Diego?

Cremation costs in San Diego vary depending on the type of service selected. Direct cremation, which includes basic care and cremation without a formal ceremony, is typically the most affordable option. Additional services such as viewing, memorial gatherings, urn selection, and death certificates will affect total pricing.

Can cremation be witnessed by the family?

Yes. Witness cremation is available for families who wish to be present when their loved one is placed into the cremation chamber. Some families choose this option for religious reasons, personal closure, or peace of mind. Witnessing is carefully coordinated to ensure privacy and compliance with California regulations. Not all crematories allow it, so advance planning is required.

Is burial at sea legal in California?

Yes. Burial at sea is legal in California but must comply with federal and state regulations. Cremated remains must be scattered at least three nautical miles from shore, and full body burial at sea requires specific preparation and offshore depth requirements. Proper documentation must also be filed with the Environmental Protection Agency.

How far offshore must ashes be scattered?

California law allows cremated remains to be scattered in the ocean as long as the ceremony takes place a minimum of 500 yards from the shoreline and outside of any harbor or enclosed bay. If the scattering is conducted three nautical miles or farther offshore, federal guidelines require that the interment be formally reported to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency within 30 days.

How much does the VA pay for funeral benefits?

The Department of Veterans Affairs provides burial allowances that vary depending on whether the death was service-related. For service-connected deaths, benefits may be higher than for non-service-related cases. Veterans buried in a national cemetery may also receive a gravesite, headstone, and military honors at no cost. Eligibility depends on discharge status and other factors.

Who qualifies for the Social Security death benefit?

The Social Security Administration provides a one-time lump sum death benefit to eligible surviving spouses or children who meet certain requirements. In addition, ongoing survivor benefits may be available depending on the deceased’s work history and family circumstances. Eligibility is based on Social Security credits earned through employment.

How quickly can a funeral home pick up a loved one?

In most cases, a licensed funeral home can respond within a few hours of being notified, depending on location and circumstances. In San Diego County, transfers from hospitals, residences, or care facilities are coordinated directly with staff on-site. Our funeral directors are available 24 hours a day to assist families when they need immediate help arranging transportation and care.

Can we delay a memorial service?

Yes. A memorial service can be held weeks or even months after cremation or burial. Many families choose to delay services to allow relatives time to travel or to plan a personalized gathering. Cremation often provides greater flexibility in scheduling. Delayed memorials may be held at a funeral home, place of worship, private residence, or even at sea.

Is green burial available in San Diego?

Yes. Green burial is available in select cemeteries and involves environmentally conscious practices such as biodegradable caskets, no embalming chemicals, and minimal vault use. Some families choose green burial to reduce environmental impact or align with personal values. Availability depends on cemetery policies and local regulations.

Can organ donation still allow for a funeral service?

Yes. Organ and tissue donation does not prevent a funeral service, viewing, or cremation in most cases. Medical professionals handle donation respectfully, and funeral arrangements can proceed afterward. Some physical limitations may affect open-casket viewing, but restorative preparation is often possible.

What is direct cremation?

Direct cremation is a simple form of cremation that takes place without a public viewing or formal ceremony beforehand. The deceased is cared for, the required permits are obtained, and the cremation is completed privately. Families may choose to hold a memorial service later. Direct cremation is often selected for its simplicity and affordability.

How long does the cremation process take?

The cremation itself typically takes several hours, depending on body weight and the crematory’s equipment. However, the overall timeline — from authorization to receiving the cremated remains — usually takes several days. This includes obtaining the death certificate, required county permits, and physician certification.

Do I need to purchase an urn?

No. Cremated remains are typically returned in a temporary container, and families may choose to keep that container or select a permanent urn later. The choice depends on whether the remains will be displayed, placed in a columbarium, buried, or scattered. Some families also select keepsake urns so multiple relatives can have a small portion.

Can I travel with cremated remains?

Yes. Cremated remains can be transported by air or ground, but airline and TSA regulations must be followed. Most airlines require the remains to be in a non-metallic container that can pass through security screening. If traveling internationally, additional documentation may be required depending on the destination country.

What happens during cremation?

Cremation is a process that reduces the body to bone fragments through controlled heat inside a cremation chamber. After the process is complete, the remaining fragments are processed into a fine consistency commonly referred to as ‘ashes’ or cremated remains. All identification and authorization procedures are carefully followed throughout to ensure proper handling.

Can cremated remains be buried?

Yes. Cremated remains may be buried in a cemetery plot, placed in a columbarium niche, interred at sea, or kept by the family. Some families choose burial to create a permanent memorial site, while others prefer scattering or private placement. Cemetery rules may vary regarding urn vaults or containers.

Can cremation take place without embalming?

Yes. Embalming is not required for cremation in California unless specific circumstances make it necessary. Most direct cremations proceed without embalming. If a public viewing is planned before cremation, embalming may be recommended but is still generally optional.

What is the difference between a funeral and a memorial service?

A funeral service typically takes place with the body present, either in a casket or prior to burial. A memorial service is held without the body present and often takes place after cremation or burial. Both can be religious or non-religious and may include readings, music, and eulogies.

How quickly must burial take place in California?

California does not require immediate burial, but the disposition must occur within a reasonable time frame. If refrigeration is used, burial can often be scheduled several days after death to allow for family travel or service planning. Cemetery availability and permit processing may also affect timing.

Can we hold a memorial service weeks or months later?

Yes. Many families choose to delay a memorial service to allow time for travel, planning, or personal reflection. Cremation often provides the greatest flexibility, but delayed services are possible with burial as well. Memorial gatherings can be held at a funeral home, place of worship, private residence, park, or even at sea.

Should young children attend a funeral?

Children can attend a funeral if the family believes it will help them understand and process the loss. Preparing children in advance by explaining what they will see and experience can reduce anxiety. Participation may help children feel included in the grieving process. However, each child’s age, maturity, and emotional readiness should be considered.

Can we hold a vigil or service at home?

Yes. In many cases, families may hold a vigil, prayer service, or small gathering at a private residence. Home services can feel intimate and meaningful. Certain legal and logistical considerations apply, particularly if the body is present, so it’s important to coordinate with a licensed funeral director.

What is a graveside service?

A graveside service is a ceremony held at the cemetery immediately before burial. It may include prayers, readings, music, military honors, or personal remarks. Graveside services can be simple and brief or more structured depending on family preferences.

Can a burial be private?

Yes. Families may choose a private burial attended only by immediate family members or invited guests. A public memorial service can still be held before or after the private interment. Funeral homes can coordinate private burial arrangements with the cemetery to ensure discretion.

What options are available for green burial?

Green burial focuses on environmentally conscious practices such as biodegradable caskets, natural burial grounds, and minimal chemical use. Some cemeteries in California offer green burial sections, though availability varies. Families choosing this option often wish to reduce environmental impact while still creating a meaningful place of remembrance.

Who qualifies for military funeral benefits?

Military funeral benefits are generally available to veterans who were discharged under conditions other than dishonorable. In most cases, individuals who served on active duty and completed their required service obligations qualify. Certain members of the National Guard and Reserves may also be eligible, depending on their service record. Documentation such as a DD Form 214 is typically required to verify eligibility.

What burial benefits does the VA provide?

The Department of Veterans Affairs may provide a gravesite in a national cemetery, opening and closing of the grave, a government headstone or marker, a burial flag, and military honors at no cost to the family. Burial allowances may also be available in certain circumstances, depending on whether the death was service-connected.

How much does the VA pay for funeral expenses?

VA burial allowances depend on the circumstances of the veteran’s passing. Service-connected deaths may qualify for higher benefits than non-service-related deaths. In some cases, reimbursement may be available for burial and funeral expenses, plot costs, or transportation. The exact amount depends on eligibility and current VA guidelines.

How do I obtain a burial flag?

A United States burial flag is provided at no cost for eligible veterans. The flag is presented to the next of kin during the funeral service as part of military honors. Funeral homes typically help request the flag from the Department of Veterans Affairs or local post office using the veteran’s discharge paperwork.

Are military honors provided at the funeral?

Yes. Eligible veterans are entitled to military funeral honors, which typically include the folding and presentation of the United States flag and the playing of ‘Taps.’ Honors are performed by members of the armed forces or authorized honor guard representatives. Arrangements must be scheduled in advance, and proof of service is required to confirm eligibility.

Can a veteran be buried at Miramar National Cemetery?

Eligible veterans and certain family members may qualify for burial at Miramar National Cemetery in San Diego. Burial in a national cemetery includes the gravesite, opening and closing of the grave, perpetual care, and a government headstone or marker at no cost. Eligibility is determined by service record and discharge status.

Are spouses and dependents eligible for national cemetery burial?

In many cases, spouses and eligible dependent children of veterans may also be interred in a national cemetery. Benefits typically include burial in the same gravesite as the veteran and a shared headstone or marker. Eligibility requirements vary, so documentation is important.

If a veteran served in more than one branch, are multiple flags provided at the service?

No. The Department of Veterans Affairs provides one U.S. burial flag per eligible veteran, regardless of how many branches they served in. The flag represents the veteran’s entire military service and sacrifice. Additional honors such as branch-specific flags, inclusion of all branches in the service program, or branch-insignia memorial items can still be arranged.

Who qualifies for the Social Security death benefit?

The Social Security Administration may provide benefits to certain surviving family members of a deceased individual who earned enough work credits. In most cases, a surviving spouse, minor children, or dependent disabled children may qualify. Because every situation is different, it is important to contact Social Security directly to confirm eligibility.

Is there a one-time Social Security death payment?

Yes. Social Security provides a one-time lump-sum death payment to an eligible surviving spouse or, in some cases, to a qualifying child. This payment is separate from any ongoing survivor benefits. Eligibility rules apply, and not all families qualify.

Can a surviving spouse receive ongoing benefits?

In some cases, a surviving spouse may qualify for monthly survivor benefits. Eligibility is generally based on age, disability status, and whether dependent children are involved. Benefits may begin at different ages and can vary depending on the deceased person’s earnings record.

How do I apply for Social Security survivor benefits?

Applications for survivor benefits must be made directly through the Social Security Administration. Families must call or schedule an appointment with a local Social Security office to begin the process. Certain documents, such as a certified death certificate and proof of relationship, may be required.

How long does it take to receive Social Security benefits?

Processing times vary depending on the type of benefit and the complexity of the claim. A one-time death payment may be processed relatively quickly once eligibility is confirmed, while ongoing survivor benefits may take additional time for review and approval.

Does the funeral home notify Social Security?

In most cases, the funeral home notifies Social Security of the death as part of the required reporting process. However, families are still responsible for contacting Social Security directly to apply for any survivor benefits. Reporting a death does not automatically initiate benefit payments.

Is burial at sea legal in California?

Yes. Burial at sea is legal in California when conducted in accordance with state and federal regulations. Cremated remains may be scattered offshore, and full body burial at sea is also permitted under specific guidelines. Proper permits and documentation are required, and certain distance requirements must be met depending on the type of interment.

How far offshore must ashes be scattered? (burial at sea)

In California, cremated remains may be scattered in the ocean as long as the ceremony takes place at least 500 yards from shore and outside of harbors and enclosed bays. If the scattering occurs three nautical miles or more offshore, federal regulations require that the interment be reported to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency within 30 days.

Can family members attend a burial at sea?

Yes. Families may attend a burial at sea ceremony aboard a licensed vessel. Services can be private and intimate or accommodate a larger gathering, depending on the charter selected. Many families choose to include readings, music, flower tributes, or military honors during the ceremony.

Is full body burial at sea allowed?

Yes. Full body burial at sea is permitted but must follow stricter preparation and depth requirements than ash scattering. The remains must be properly prepared for ocean interment, and specific federal regulations apply. Coordination with a licensed funeral director and an experienced sea burial provider is essential to ensure compliance.

What paperwork is required for burial at sea?

Required documentation typically includes a certified death certificate and a disposition permit issued by the county. Additional reporting may be required depending on the type of burial and distance offshore. When federal reporting applies, notification must be submitted within the designated time frame.

Can ashes be scattered without a chartered boat?

Yes. Families may scatter cremated remains from a private vessel, provided all legal distance requirements are followed. Remains must not be scattered from bridges, piers, or within enclosed bays. Even when arranging a private scattering, it is important to ensure compliance with California and federal guidelines.
